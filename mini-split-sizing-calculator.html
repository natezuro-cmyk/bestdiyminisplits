    How to size a mini split — the homeowner's version

    The single biggest mistake DIY mini split shoppers make is buying too big. A 24,000 BTU unit in a 500 sq ft room sounds like "more power = better," but oversized units short-cycle: they blast cold air for 5 minutes, shut off, and the room's humidity never gets pulled down. The room feels clammy, your electric bill is higher, and the compressor lifespan drops. Under-sizing is worse — the unit just runs 24/7 and never catches up on a hot day.

    Right-sizing matters, and the sq-ft rules of thumb you'll find on Amazon listings aren't enough. Here's what actually moves the number:

    The 5 factors that change BTU sizing

    1. Square footage. Baseline: ~20 BTU per sq ft in a mixed US climate. Multiply by ceiling height if taller than 8 ft.
    2. Climate zone. Hot & humid climates (Florida, Texas Gulf Coast) need ~25 BTU/ft² for cooling. Cold climates (Minnesota, Maine) need 40–55 BTU/ft² for heating — and heat usually dictates size in a heat pump.
    3. Insulation. A drafty 1920s farmhouse needs 20% more capacity. A new-build with sprayed foam walls needs 15% less.
    4. Sun exposure. A south/west-facing room with big windows gains heat all afternoon — add 10–20%.
    5. Occupants & heat-generating equipment. Each adult over 2 adds ~600 BTU of latent load. Kitchens add 4,000 BTU for cooking loads.

    Why heating usually drives the size

    A mini split is a heat pump — same unit does both cooling and heating by reversing the refrigeration cycle. In any climate cooler than Atlanta, the heating load is larger than the cooling load, so the heat output requirement is what determines the unit size. This is why our calculator computes both and recommends the larger number.

    Rule of thumb: in zones 4–7, if you're replacing a furnace with a mini split, size based on heating BTU and confirm the model maintains capacity at your 99% design temperature (the coldest 1% of winter hours in your zip code).
